BACK TO BITE US IN THE BUTT?

The frequency of shark attacks has been rising considerably the past few years and the risk of being attacked by a shark is higher than it has ever been in history.
The question is why?

The obvious answer for this rise in attacks is population growth.

Human's exponential population growth simply means that there are more humans on Earth for sharks to attack.

This is what most articles and news stories will posit as the main reason for the increase in incidents.

But…that can't be it, right? Especially since most shark attacks occur less than 100 feet from shore.

Sharks come near shore for various reasons, and it is not because human population is getting larger.

Why Sharks Travel Near Shore

Sharks are cold-blooded creatures, so they will migrate to areas with warmer water temperatures.

The overall warming of the ocean's water draws more sharks towards shore.

In fact, the change in the ocean's temperature has been so drastic that sharks have abandoned their seasonal migrations.

Sharks often go near shores for nursing purposes.

They may feel their territory is under threat with so many humans doing human activity things and go on the offensive.

Most of the time, however, they are near shore searching for food.

Aside from being ideal hunting grounds, researchers also speculate that the fish carcasses left behind by fishing industries attract them closer to shore.

Sharks are not craving human flesh. In fact, they very much dislike us and would rather not eat us.

A majority of shark attacks are of the “bite and release” nature

A majority of shark attacks are of the “bite and release” nature. Humans floating out in the ocean can be mistaken as prey.

The shark will attack, realize that it has attacked a human. Since sharks don’t fancy human flesh, it lets go and does not attack further.

The USA composes ~38% of total shark attacks with a total of 2336. AUSTRALIA composes ~21.4% of total shark attacks with a total of 1299. SOUTH AFRICA composes ~9.3% of total shark attacks with a total of 564.

MEXICO, ITALY, NEW CALEDONIA, PHILIPPINES, REUNION, and FIJI have recorded over 50 incidents within this time frame. Other locations that have shark attack records do not break a count of 50, and account for 0.017-0.73% of total incidents

Combined, the USA, AUSTRALIA, and SOUTH AFRICA account for over half of the total recorded shark attack incidents. Again, it is easy to posit population growth as the primary reason for the increase of shark attacks, but there are other factors at play here that need to be considered.

Conclusion
The numbers look big and scary, but it is important to keep in mind that shark attacks are still extremely rare. Remember that humans kill millions of sharks every year. In fact, the sharks in our oceans are at risk of extinction. It is completely understandable to be afraid of getting attacked by a shark. One moment you are having a lovely day at the beach, the next you are bleeding out because a shark mistook you for food. But we cannot deny that human activity is impacting sharks and their behavior, leading to increased risk of attacks. While it is scary, the fear should not be turned towards sharks themselves. Ultimately, we should be afraid of our own actions because our activity is making the risk of being attacked by a shark higher than it has ever been in history.
